DO NOT joke around... when I AM ASKING

If someone sends you this link while asking a question, it implies that you should not... MAKE JOKES ABOUT THEIR QUESTION

What defines "joking around" in this context?

Joking isn't just about telling jokes. In technical discussions, "joking around" includes:

  • Bait-and-switch (Mock Seriousness): Deliberately creating a message that looks long and professional, but the content is actually misleading, harmful, or nonsense (e.g., recommending fake or malicious tools).
  • Dismissive Sarcasm: Mocking the asker's idea as "delusional" or "useless," instead of explaining why it might not work. If they ask "How to do it," don't answer with "Why would you do that?" just to sound superior.
  • Visual Spam: Sending memes, stickers, or GIFs that serve no purpose in explaining the issue.
  • Troll Links: Rick Rolls, Duckrolls, or any link leading to entertainment content meant to derail the conversation.
  • ... and any action that disrupts the train of thought of the person needing help.

The Consequences?

  • Lost in Translation: Not everyone shares your cultural context or humor style. A "friendly jab" can easily be misinterpreted as a personal insult.
  • Adding Fuel to the Fire: Unintentionally making the situation more tense, especially when the asker is highly stressed (e.g., facing a deadline or a production outage).
  • Signal-to-Noise Ratio Drop: You are diluting the thread. Serious answers get buried under memes, making it harder for others to help.
  • Loss of Professionalism: Breaking the necessary focus, even in "casual" community spaces.

"You must be fun at parties..." / "Why so serious?"

You might feel that asking for "no jokes" is an attack on your freedom of speech or your right to have fun.

But consider this: If you have the freedom to joke, the asker also has the freedom to set a boundary for their own inquiry.

Is that request unreasonable?
No. It is a reasonable boundary. They are here for a solution, not for entertainment.

If this request annoys you, it‚Äôs likely a clash of communication styles rather than anyone being "wrong." In that case, the mature move is to:

  • Quietly ignore the thread.
  • Hide the message or block the user.

... instead of forcing your humor onto someone who clearly hasn't consented to it.

Why do people send this link?

  • Burnout & Fatigue: They may have received too many unhelpful, vague, or "troll" answers in the past. They are just tired of filtering through the noise.
  • High Stakes: They might be in a situation where a mistake has serious real-world consequences, and they can't afford ambiguity.
  • Cultural Context: They might be from a culture where workplace/technical seriousness is the norm and "roasting" is considered disrespectful.

What if the question is unclear / lacks data / looks like a joke?

You should still answer politely! If you suspect it's a "low-effort" question, guide them on how to ask better.

If you want to make fun of it, keep it to your private DMs or groups where the asker is not present. Publicly mocking a beginner is just gatekeeping.

On "Internet Culture" & Reading the Room

If you are used to Western internet culture (Reddit, Discord, etc.), sarcasm and irony might be your default language. But remember:

  • The Internet is Global: What is a "sick burn" in one culture can be a "slap in the face" in another. In many Asian cultures, preserving "face" and hierarchy is crucial. Aggressive roasting is often seen as pure hostility, not bonding.
  • Context is King: Even if the asker shares your culture, stress kills humor. When someone is panicking over a bug, your "funny" meme feels like you're laughing at their misfortune.

"Please don't forget: timing decides whether you are relieving stress, or if you are just being a jerk."

Respect the boundary. When someone explicitly asks for seriousness, it's not the time to show off your wit. It's time to show your professionalism.

Why did I write this?

Since I was young, I've often been the victim of ill-timed jokes. Even when I was focused on work and in no mood to laugh, I was still poked and prodded. To them, it was "just a joke," but to me, it was pure exhaustion.

Even within my own family, sarcastic remarks from relatives often silenced me. But in the name of so-called "family harmony," I was forced to smile and answer things I didn't want to.

When going online, I just hoped to find a space to ask and answer decently. But instead, what I usually got were memes, malicious tools, brainrot videos, or irrelevant answers thrown straight in my face.

No one had made this website yet, so I did. It's just that simple. What we need is **Signal**, not **Noise**. We need productivity and respect, not rude interruptions.

That is the reason nohello.net was born, and I am simply here to continue that legacy.
